<p>A <b>ford</b> is a shallow place with good footing where a <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">river</a> or <a href="/wiki/Stream" title="Stream">stream</a> may be crossed by wading or inside a vehicle.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> A ford is mostly a natural phenomenon, in contrast to a <a href="/wiki/Low_water_crossing" title="Low water crossing">low water crossing</a>, which is an artificial bridge that allows crossing a river or stream when water is low.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ford_(crossing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A ford is a much cheaper form of river crossing than a <a href="/wiki/Bridge" title="Bridge">bridge</a>, but it may become impassable after heavy rain or during flood conditions. A ford is therefore normally only suitable for very minor roads (and for paths intended for walkers and horse riders etc.). Most modern fords are usually shallow enough to be crossed by <a href="/wiki/Automobile" title="Automobile" class="mw-redirect">cars</a> and other wheeled or tracked vehicles (a process known as "fording").</p>
<p>In New Zealand, however, fords are a normal part of major roads, including, until 2010, along <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_State_Highway_1" title="New Zealand State Highway 1">State Highway 1</a> on the South Island's east coast.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> As most inter-city domestic passengers travel by air and as much cargo goes by sea, long distance road traffic is low and fords are thus a practical necessity<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (August 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> for crossing seasonal rivers. In dry weather, drivers become aware of a ford by crunching across outwash detritus on the roadway. A <a href="/wiki/Bailey_bridge" title="Bailey bridge">Bailey bridge</a> may be built off the main line of the road to carry emergency traffic during high water.</p>
<p>At places where the water is shallow enough, but the material on the riverbed will not support heavy vehicles, fords are sometimes improved by building a submerged <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a> floor. In such cases a <a href="/wiki/Curb_(road)" title="Curb (road)" class="mw-redirect">curb</a> (kerb) is often placed on the downstream side to prevent vehicles slipping off, as growth of <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a> will often make the slab very slippery. Fords may be also equipped with a post indicating the water depth, so that users may know if the water is too deep to attempt to cross. Some have an adjacent footbridge so that pedestrians may cross without getting their feet wet.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Watersplash">Watersplash</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ford_(crossing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Watersplash">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Rye_Water_Ford,_Dalry.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Rye_Water_Ford%2C_Dalry.JPG/220px-Rye_Water_Ford%2C_Dalry.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Rye_Water_Ford%2C_Dalry.JPG/330px-Rye_Water_Ford%2C_Dalry.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Rye_Water_Ford%2C_Dalry.JPG/440px-Rye_Water_Ford%2C_Dalry.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2288" data-file-height="1712" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Rye_Water_Ford,_Dalry.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Rye Water Ford in North Ayrshire, a rare unmodernised crossing of a minor river</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A road running below the water level of a stream or river is often known as a "watersplash". It is a common name for a ford or stretch of wet road in some areas, and sometimes also used to describe tidal crossings. They have become a common feature in <a href="/wiki/Rallying" title="Rallying">rallying</a> courses. There are enthusiasts who seek out and drive through these water features, recording details (such as wave created, position and access) on dedicated websites.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>There are many old fords known as watersplashes in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>. Examples are at <a href="/wiki/Brockenhurst" title="Brockenhurst">Brockenhurst</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hampshire" title="Hampshire">Hampshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wookey" title="Wookey">Wookey</a> in <a href="/wiki/Somerset" title="Somerset">Somerset</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Swinbrook" title="Swinbrook">Swinbrook</a> in <a href="/wiki/Oxfordshire" title="Oxfordshire">Oxfordshire</a>. Some of these are being replaced by bridges as these are a more reliable form of crossing in adverse weather conditions.</p>
<p>The <a href="//commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Deanford.JPG" class="extiw" title="commons:File:Deanford.JPG">Dean Ford</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kilmarnock" title="Kilmarnock">Kilmarnock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ayrshire" title="Ayrshire">Ayrshire</a>, is mentioned in the <a href="/wiki/Deed" title="Deed">deeds</a> of this<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words" class="mw-redirect"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too vague attribution or weasel words. (August 2015)">which?</span></a></i>]</sup> property, which was gifted to the local people. The ford has had to be maintained as a property boundary feature, despite several cars a year being washed away.</p>
<p>Not just a British phenomenon, some very spectacular versions of the watersplash feature can be found in diverse locations. <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> has the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Savannah" title="Gulf Savannah" class="mw-redirect">Gulf Savannah</a>, and others may be found in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>. They are also found on some <a href="/wiki/Tennessee" title="Tennessee">Tennessee</a> backroads, where they are referred to as "underwater bridges".</p>
<p>In Israel and part of the British areas under the <a href="/wiki/British_Mandate_for_Palestine_(legal_instrument)" title="British Mandate for Palestine (legal instrument)">mandate</a> a low water crossing or watersplash had been known as "Irish bridge"<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> in reference to the <a href="/wiki/Irish_War_of_Independence" title="Irish War of Independence">Anglo–Irish war</a><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Location_names">Location names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ford_(crossing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Location names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The names of many towns and villages are derived from the word 'ford'; for example, <a href="/wiki/Oxford" title="Oxford">Oxford</a> (a ford where <a href="/wiki/Ox" title="Ox">oxen</a> crossed the river: see the <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms_of_Oxford" title="Coat of arms of Oxford">Oxford coat of arms</a>), or <a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Stratford" class="extiw" title="wikt:Stratford">Stratford</a> (a ford on a <a href="/wiki/Roman_road" title="Roman road" class="mw-redirect">Roman road</a>). Similarly, the <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> word <i>Furt</i> (as in <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt" title="Frankfurt">Frankfurt</a>, the ford of the <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Franks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ochsenfurt" title="Ochsenfurt">Ochsenfurt</a>, synonymous to Oxford, <a href="/wiki/Schweinfurt" title="Schweinfurt">Schweinfurt</a>, a ford where <a href="/wiki/Swine" title="Swine" class="mw-redirect">swine</a> crossed the river, and <a href="/wiki/Klagenfurt" title="Klagenfurt">Klagenfurt</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a> <i>voorde</i>, (as in <a href="/wiki/Vilvoorde" title="Vilvoorde">Vilvoorde</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coevorden" title="Coevorden">Coevorden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zandvoort" title="Zandvoort">Zandvoort</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Amersfoort" title="Amersfoort">Amersfoort</a>) are <a href="/wiki/Cognate" title="Cognate">cognates</a> and have the same meaning.</p>
<p>Towns such as <a href="/wiki/Maastricht" title="Maastricht">Maastricht</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dordrecht" title="Dordrecht">Dordrecht</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Utrecht" title="Utrecht">Utrecht</a> also formed at fords. The ending <i>tricht, drecht</i>, or <i>trecht</i> is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> word <i>traiectum</i>, meaning "crossing". Thus the name Utrecht, originally the Roman fort of <a href="/wiki/Traiectum_(Utrecht)" title="Traiectum (Utrecht)">Traiectum</a>, is derived from "Uut Trecht", meaning "downstream crossing". The Afrikaans form was taken into South African English as <i>drift</i> and led to place names like Rorke's Drift. Similarly, in Slavic languages, the word <a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/brod" class="extiw" title="wikt:brod">brod</a> comes from the linguistic root that means "river-crossing" or "place where a river can be crossed". Although today "brod" in the Croatian language means "ship", <a href="/wiki/Slavonski_Brod" title="Slavonski Brod">Slavonski Brod</a> in Croatia, as well as <a href="/wiki/Makedonski_Brod" title="Makedonski Brod">Makedonski Brod</a> in <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Macedonia" title="Republic of Macedonia">Macedonia</a> and other place names containing "Brod" in Slavic countries, where "brod" is still the word for ford, are named after fords.</p>
